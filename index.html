Planning and executing a focused Sprint that actually addresses a blocking area and helps move an Open Source Project forward

We will discuss this in general, using getting Drupal 8 released as an example

  • Background: Drupal 8 release plans
  • Examples of successful sprints: Ghent, New Jersey
  • Planning the sprint
  • What should happen at the sprint
  • D8 Accelerate Program

structure of the talk:
30 minutes presentation,
30 mintues discussion.
please write down your thoughts and questions during,
so you will remember them.

audience

  • decision makers in organizations that want to do something to help release happen faster, want to host or sponsor a sprint
  • local organizers that want to do something to help release happen faster, want to know if them holding a sprint would help, and how it might be different than organizing their typical events. or thinking about adding a focused sprint to a camp.
  • contributors want to know what happens at this kind of sprint, want to know if attending is something they should try and do, and what it would be like
  • contributors to other free software projects who want insight into what the Drupal is doing and how it works
  • organizers of past focused sprints who want to add their knowledge
  • contrib maintainers who are thinking about D8 upgrade or feature sprints
  • project managers who think their skills are not needed, but who could contribute to getting release to happen faster

We think you will get something valuable out of the talk.

  • * not-yet contributors, have heard of sprints, want to know what the big deal is about sprints
  • * business, sales, and marketing people who are not making the money decisions

Planning

Planning is a huge part of the success of sprints.

topic identifying goals

short term, urgent, timely

triaging issues ahead of time

  • discover identifying issues
  • understand the issues that you know about
  • identify issues that block other work and fix them before sprint
  • updating the issues

Example

  • week -3
    • scoping focus
    • identify and invite attendees
  • week -2
    • 2 hangouts 1-1.5 hours
  • week -1
    • hangout
    • have initial debates
    • so that the first part is not with people coming up to speed
    • 1-2 people domain experts already start working on issues, blockers, prerequisite, choke points
  • Hours and Funding
    • hours
      • logistics, big idea, plans: 9 hours * 4
      • actually working on issues: 10 hours * 4
    • example funding
      • grant
      • camp (comp'd tickets, event food)
      • airplane/hotel: daniel, wim, alex, jess, tim
      • travelish/hotel: kalpana, matt

Have a committer

  • advance buy-in
  • during: remotely dedicated or on-site

At the Sprint

new section of at the sprint stuffs.

track key issues

  • their status
  • what is blocking them
  • who is working on them (can be more than one person)
  • what remains to be done to call the issue done

make sure updates get posted on the issue

(patches, questions, answers, documenting conversaitons)

  • during the day
  • before (leaving for) dinner
  • because those people's future selves (the next day) will be thankful, and also, there are other people around the world that should have access to the information in the room (sometimes they work on and review an issue) whild the on-site group is eating, taking a break, or sleeping. they cannot if updates are not posted in the canonical public place: the issue

check-ins during the day

  • check-in with people during the day - what do they need to be productive
  • check on the testbots
  • get pictures

daily update

  • makes final report much easier
  • keeps attendees up to speed (even they can not know all that happens in the room each day)
  • humans: celebrate success
  • makes spectators excited
  • a person owns doing it, done by a person with high level participation in everything, but maybe not the technical lead

Follow-up

communicating what happened

  • sometimes required by the grant
  • funded by company so they get return on investment
  • enriching the community knowledge

check on issues that got close but not committed

negotiate the terms for rtbc/commit for them (could be a core committer, the lead of the sprint, or the project manager for the sprint)

Controversial things

  • seed the group early (before you pick a date) with key people
    • closer to release more important
    • make a list of you must have people
    • the first people to confirm can have effect on the date of the event. make sure they will be there.
    • organizer (or maybe one of the first key people confirmed) has an existing open communication channel with a committer
    • there is a time period of picking the date, where the organizer is temperature test if it is possible they could come. and then there is a time where the organizers confirms people can really attend. for sure.
    • publishing the names of individuals who have confirmed to attend will affect the next round of key people say they will attend
  • a person planning has an existing open communication channel with a committer
  • at least one person confirmed to attend should have a history of good communication with the committer
  • ask particular people to attend
  • do not invite distracting or difficult people with a history/pattern of disruptive behavior
  • say no. it is ok to exclude people based on skill level. redirect them to another event that will be a better match for them
  • provide an environment for focusing, do not mix with sprints that are on other topics or are focused on mentoring new people
  • 2 out of 10 had experience contributing (to the project (core)) but not yet key domain specialists. further away from release, ratio can be more weighted toward people with less domain specialities, and newer contributors
  • people being invited have to trust that the lead or organizer is not going to make a disaster of things. that the event will be comfortable, and worth the time. investing days of what might be unpaid work is a big risky investment
  • current demands or organizing a sprint and being the on site sprint lead and documenting and following up after: put tooooooo much pressure on one person to be good at all the things
  • project manager for the sprint. past successful sprints have had a key person who is both a domain expert and has project manager skills. When it is the same person, it puts more demands and stress on a person. In the same way we want experienced contributors, we need project managers with experience with core.
    • what might be the cost of paying? $100*8 * number of days. plus airplane and hotel. $3200 + $600 + $120 * 4 = $4300
  • *moar* logistics handling by someone, the DA?
  • expert logistical staffing
  • dont make volunteers bitter, burned out, and sad. we can do good for the project and do good for the people
  • money!
  • humans. things that some people think should not make a difference but need to recognize that do
  • allowing for the expectation that at a sprint, someone might show up at 10am and leave at 5pm and be ok with that. not expecting 20 hours of work
  • after sprint work. consensus can be formed during the sprint and work continues after. sometimes unpaid
